How to Add Weight to a Pickleball Paddle

B
Braydon · Pickleball Effect · April 6, 2026
Adding weight to a pickleball paddle changes how it plays — more power on drives, more stability on resets, less vibration on off-center hits. Tungsten tape is the most precise way to do it. This is exactly how I do it.

A lot of players buy a new paddle when they're actually just a few strips of tape away from fixing what bothers them. If your paddle feels too light, deflects too easily, or lacks punch on drives, adding weight is worth trying before spending $200 on a new one.

Tungsten tape is the standard for a reason — it's dense, it stays put, and you can remove it cleanly if you don't like the result. Here's the full process.

Why Add Weight to a Pickleball Paddle?

Weight affects three things: power, stability, and maneuverability. More weight means more mass behind the ball on drives, more resistance to twisting on off-center hits, and more dampening of vibration. Less weight means faster hand speed, easier resets, and better maneuverability at the kitchen.

The tradeoff is real — but most players who complain about a paddle feeling "floaty" or "unstable" are under-weighted, not over-weighted. The default weight range on most paddles (7.6–8.2 oz) is conservative. A lot of intermediate and advanced players end up in the 8.3–8.8 oz range after tuning.

Where You Add WeightWhat It Changes
12 o'clock (top edge)More power & swing weight. Slower hands, harder drives.
3 & 9 o'clock (side edges)More stability, wider sweet spot. Less twisting on off-center hits.
Handle / butt capMore swing weight from the back. Power without changing balance point.
Throat / lower faceAdds weight without changing balance, minimal impact on feel.

The most common setup: 1 strip at 3 o'clock, 1 strip at 9 o'clock. Adds ~2–4 grams, widens the sweet spot, improves reset stability. Good starting point for almost any player.

What You Need

Not much. You need tape, scissors, and clean hands. That's it.

  • Tungsten tape — denser than lead tape, no health concerns, easier to cut cleanly. PE Tungsten Tape strips are pre-cut for pickleball paddles.
  • Scissors or a craft knife — for custom lengths. Pre-cut strips skip this.
  • A scale (optional but useful) — knowing your before/after weight helps you dial in future adjustments. Any kitchen scale down to 0.1g works.
  • Isopropyl alcohol & a cloth — clean the paddle surface first. Tape sticks better and peels cleaner later.
Tip: Weigh your paddle before you start. Note the static weight and, if you have a swing weight tool, the swing weight. That baseline makes it much easier to evaluate whether the change actually helped.

Placement Guide

3 & 9 o'clock — The Universal Starting Point

One strip on each side edge, centered at the widest part of the paddle. This adds stability without meaningfully changing your swing weight or balance. If you've never tuned a paddle before, start here. Most players find this alone is enough.

12 o'clock — More Power, Less Speed

A strip across the top edge shifts the balance point toward the head and increases swing weight. You'll feel more mass behind drives and overheads. Hand speed at the kitchen will drop slightly. Best for players who already have fast hands and want more punch on power shots.

Handle / Butt Cap

Adding weight inside the handle or under the overgrip at the butt increases swing weight without shifting the balance point forward. The paddle will feel heavier overall but head-neutral. Some players prefer this for maintaining maneuverability while still gaining mass.

Avoid the face of the paddle. Tape on the hitting surface affects ball contact, disrupts spin generation, and may violate USAPA tournament rules. Keep tape to edges and the handle only.

Step-by-Step: How to Apply Tungsten Tape

1

Clean the surface

Wipe the edge where you'll place the tape with isopropyl alcohol. Let it dry completely — 30 seconds is enough. Any oil or residue from handling will reduce adhesion and cause peeling later.

2

Weigh your paddle (optional)

Put it on a scale and note the static weight. If you have a swing weight tool, record that too. This gives you a before/after comparison and helps you dial future adjustments.

3

Cut or size your strips

For 3 and 9 o'clock placement, a strip roughly 2–3 inches is standard. Pre-cut strips from PE are sized for this. If cutting your own, use sharp scissors for a clean edge — jagged cuts peel faster.

4

Apply the tape

Peel the backing and press the strip firmly onto the edge, centered at the position you've chosen. Press along the full length — work from the middle outward to avoid air bubbles. For edge placement, wrap slightly over both sides of the edge for better adhesion.

5

Press and smooth

Use your thumbnail or the back of a pen to press the tape down firmly. Pay attention to the edges of the strip — those are where peeling starts. Press for 10–15 seconds on each strip.

6

Test and adjust

Play with it. Give it a full session before making changes — your hands need time to adapt. If it feels too heavy, peel one strip. If you want more, add another. The process is reversible; don't overthink the first application.

Tips & Common Mistakes

Start lighter than you think

Two strips at 3 and 9 feels like nothing in your hand before you play. On court it's noticeable. Most players overshoot on their first attempt — add one strip, play a session, then decide if you want more.

Don't layer tape over old tape

Peeling and re-applying is better than stacking. Layered tape creates uneven weight distribution and the bottom layer will pull the top layer off eventually. Clean application every time.

Use edge guard tape on top of tungsten tape

If you play on outdoor courts or hit the ground occasionally, a layer of PE Edge Guard Tape over the tungsten tape protects the strips and extends their life. The tungsten tape stays put and the edge guard takes the impact.

The sweet spot test: After adding weight, deliberately hit 10 balls slightly off-center — above and below your normal contact point, left and right. A properly weighted paddle at 3 and 9 should show clearly improved consistency on the off-center hits compared to before.

Swing weight vs. static weight — know the difference

Static weight is what a scale reads. Swing weight is how heavy the paddle feels in motion — determined by where the weight is distributed, not just how much there is. Two paddles at 8.2 oz can swing completely differently. Adding weight at 12 o'clock increases swing weight significantly; adding the same weight to the handle barely changes it. Know what you're trying to change.
